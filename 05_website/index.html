Green Berlin

Tangible data visualization of green areas and water in Berlin.

Most visualizations use paper's or screen's two-dimensional plane, even when visualizing spatial (three-dimensional) data. Green Berlin explores the opportunity space of tangible artefacts, created through rapid prototyping techniques, like in this case a laser cutter. As we are multi-sensory beings, the physical, the haptic world gives us a certain sensation we cannot deny. While the virtual realm allows us to create complex multidimensional visualizations, a real 3D object, even though lower in its complexity still has a high appeal.

Germany's greenest cities - Berliner Morgenpost. Link

Berlin only ranked number 63 on Germany's greenest cities (Berliner Morgenpost), it still offers a lot of parks, rivers and lakes for its citizens to enjoy. One of the reasons why Berlin is a great city to be in summertime. Due to scale and technical limitations the physical map could not show every park and pond in Berlin, but even the selection already paints quite a green picture.


Laser cutter engraving the water layer.
OpenStreetMap's Overpass-API was used to aggregate the data. Small regions were removed programmatically and the polygons were slightly optimized for speeding up the cutting process. While we used a intersect algorithm to remove all features outside of the Berlin border, we needed to manually tweak the map at the borders, as there are several features directly on the border of Berlin, which we wanted to keep. Most laser printers accept DXF files (vectors) for cutting and raster files for engraving. Both can be exported from most vector-editing applications as well as QGIS.

The green and water layer as well as the border of Berlin.
After sending the data off to the printer and waiting for a couple of hours, the map was glued to a board and thoroughly impregnated, to withstand the watering to come. The next step took us to the woods of Berlin collecting all sorts of moss. The moss was then cut into shape and then glued to the board (we used children's glue, the least toxic glue we could find). More than a month and daily sprinkles of water later, the map is still alive (well most of it).
